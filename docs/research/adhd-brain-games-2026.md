# ADHD Brain-Break Mini-Games — Research (2026)

Scope: evidence check for a "brain break" mini-games section in Kairo. Goal is
honest framing, not clinical claims.

## 1. Cognitive mini-games — what evidence actually says

- **N-back (working memory):** Best-evidenced of the bunch. A 2025 study
  (MDPI/PMC12468938) found adults with ADHD doing adaptive dual n-back ~18
  sessions/1 month improved WAIS-IV working-memory scores (d≈0.46), but gains
  were verbal > spatial, sample excluded comorbidities, and durability past
  the study window is unproven. Broader n-back meta-analyses find near-transfer
  (getting better at n-back) but weak/no far-transfer to real life. **Verdict:
  legitimate as a fun working-memory challenge; don't claim real-life benefit.**
- **Stroop (interference/attention):** Well-validated as an ADHD *research and
  screening* task (robust group differences, PMC1988818); gamified versions
  hold up psychometrically (PMC11043929) and engage low-attention users well.
  It's a measurement tool first, training tool a distant second — frame as
  "test your focus," not "train your focus."
- **Reaction-time games:** No credible evidence these improve real-world
  attention; just fun/quick with a clean personal-best number. Fine as a
  low-stakes break, not a cognitive intervention.
- **Memory pairs / matching:** Popular and enjoyable, plausible near-transfer
  to short-term/visual memory, but most supporting sources are wellness
  blogs, not RCTs — "harmless fun with plausible mild benefit," not proven.
- **General "brain training" industry:** In 2016 the FTC fined Lumos Labs
  (Lumosity) $2M for deceptive claims that games delay dementia, improve
  school/work performance, or help ADHD/PTSD — no science behind those
  claims, only "you get better at the game itself." A 2014 consensus
  statement from 70+ neuroscientists criticized the industry's exaggerated
  marketing broadly. **This is the cautionary tale Kairo must not repeat.**

**Bottom line:** honest copy = "quick, fun mental warm-ups" / "give your brain
a break," never "improves focus," "trains your ADHD brain," or "clinically
proven."

## 2. Time perception — is a "guess the seconds" game on-brand?

Yes — it's one of the most replicated ADHD findings, and using it as a game
is honest (it's testing/practicing a known ADHD-relevant skill, not claiming
therapy).

- Barkley's model (1997/1998): time-perception deficits stem from impaired
  behavioral inhibition/executive function, not a standalone "clock" deficit.
- Bauermeister & Barkley: ADHD kids showed larger errors in time
  **reproduction** than time **estimation** — reproducing a duration (e.g.
  holding a button for as long as a remembered interval) is the more
  ADHD-sensitive task type vs. verbally guessing a duration.
- Meta-analysis (Zheng et al. 2022) confirms a general time-perception deficit
  across children/adolescents with ADHD; adult findings are mixed (Medical
  Science Monitor Czech study, PMC review) — effect is real but noisier in
  adults.
- Typical research durations: **short seconds-range intervals** — 3, 5, 6, 12,
  15, 17, 24, 30, 36, 45, 48, 60 seconds show up across studies. Accuracy
  degrades as duration grows, and studies often mix a few short (5–12s) and
  longer (30–60s) trials to see where errors emerge. Under ~1s (millisecond
  range) is a *different, less-impaired* mechanism (Nature Sci Rep 2022) — so
  don't build the game around sub-second timing, use seconds.

**Recommendation:** a "how long was that?" reproduction game (hold/tap when
you think N seconds have passed, or judge which of two intervals was longer)
is legitimate and on-brand. Use it framed as reflective/fun ("your brain's
inner clock, tested"), not diagnostic.

## 3. What makes micro-games engaging for ADHD without becoming a scroll trap

- **Immediate, visible feedback** is the core driver — ADHD brains respond
  more to immediate than delayed reward; every action needs an instant
  visual reaction (imaginovation.net; adhdcentre.co.uk).
- **Right-sized difficulty**, not too easy/hard — best implementations
  auto-adjust difficulty in real time (e.g., EndeavorRx) rather than fixed
  levels.
- **Personal-best / progress-over-time beats leaderboards** for this
  audience — competitive leaderboards (e.g., Opal) can create pressure/
  comparison anxiety; ADHD-focused design favors visible personal progress +
  light structure (Habitica-style small wins) over social competition.
- **Hard session caps matter** — anti-doomscroll research is explicit: ADHD's
  novelty-seeking + variable reward is exactly what makes infinite scroll
  sticky, so a brain-break feature needs a built-in *end*.
- **Offer contextually, not as a destination** — position as an option
  *during* natural gaps (after finishing a timer/task, in transition, while
  waiting), not a tab users browse to and lose time in.
- **Stopping cues**: a visible round counter ("2 of 3"), a natural
  summary screen at the end, no auto-advance to "one more round."

## 4. What comparable apps do — and what users complain about

- **Inflow** (CBT-based ADHD app, $22–48/mo): rich in reading/journaling
  content but explicitly **lacks game/reward mechanics** — users ask for more
  game-like elements. Complaints: too much reading for an ADHD audience,
  aggressive trial-to-paid billing, 7-day trial too short for this population.
- **Finch** (self-care pet): praised by ND users because caring for a bird
  makes small actions (breathing, journaling, check-ins) feel lighter than a
  bare checklist — "full marks for ADHD friendliness," a "dopamine machine"
  via rewards on every task + cosmetics. Worth borrowing: **wrap the reward
  in something with personality**, not just a numeric score.
- **Joon** (kids/family gamified chores): virtual pet + coin economy for
  parent-assigned quests; parents report kids self-starting tasks. Same
  pattern — pet care as the *carrier* for gamification, not the game itself.
- **Lumosity / Peak**: cautionary tale (see §1) — FTC-fined for overclaiming
  real-world/clinical benefit, and commonly criticized for aggressive
  subscription paywalls gating nearly all games behind premium. **Avoid**:
  hard paywalling brain breaks (should feel like a free, low-stakes perk) and
  any "improves your ADHD" copy.

## 5. Breathing / grounding micro-exercises

- **Box breathing (4-4-4-4):** inhale 4s, hold 4s, exhale 4s, hold 4s. Extends
  exhale relative to inhale → vagal/parasympathetic activation, lowers heart
  rate and cortisol. One of the two fastest, most research-backed calming
  techniques alongside 5-4-3-2-1.
- **4-7-8 breathing:** inhale 4s, hold 7s, exhale 8s. Small trials (e.g.
  ClinicalTrials.gov NCT05492929, NCT07303634) show anxiety/sleep-quality
  benefit, but evidence is "limited scientific research, considerable
  anecdotal support" — genuinely relaxing, not a proven clinical intervention.
- **5-4-3-2-1 grounding:** sensory-anchoring technique (5 things you see, 4
  hear, 3 touch, 2 smell, 1 taste), **~2–4 minutes** for full effect. Good for
  acute overwhelm/panic; explicitly "an in-the-moment coping tool, not a
  treatment."
- ADHD-specific: breathwork is a helpful *adjunct* alongside ADHD/anxiety
  treatment, not a replacement — same "coping tool, not therapy" framing.

## Recommended copy guidelines

- Say: "quick brain breaks," "a fun little challenge," "stretch your focus for
  a minute," "see your personal best," "a moment to reset."
- Never say: "improves your ADHD," "trains your brain," "clinically proven,"
  "boosts executive function," "treats symptoms." Kairo is a planner with fun
  breaks, not a CBT/neurofeedback product — don't blur that line even
  implicitly.
- If citing a game's basis (e.g. "based on the Stroop effect" or "inspired by
  ADHD time-perception research"), it's fine to name the concept for
  credibility/interest, but pair it with a soft disclaimer tone ("just for
  fun," "no pressure") rather than efficacy claims.
- Always give an exit: a visible "skip" and a natural end screen — never
  auto-queue another round.

## Per-game recommendations

| Game | Rounds/Length | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Time-estimation/reproduction** | 3 rounds, target durations mixed from **5s, 12s, 30s** (short + mid range, matches research spread); ~30–45s total | "Tap when you think N seconds passed" or "hold for N seconds"; show actual vs. guessed, delta in seconds, personal-best streak of closest guesses |
| **Reaction-time** | 5 taps/round, 1 round (~15–20s) | Random-delay stimulus, show avg + best ms; personal-best only, no leaderboard |
| **Memory pairs** | 6–8 cards (3–4 pairs) for a "break" size, 1 round (~30–60s) | Keep grid small — this is a break, not a puzzle session; show move-count + time |
| **Breathing exercise** | Box breathing default, 4 cycles (~64s total: 4×16s) or 5-4-3-2-1 as a 2–3 min alt for higher overwhelm | Offer both a "quick" (box, ~1 min) and "longer" (5-4-3-2-1, ~2–4 min) option; no round/score framing at all — this one should feel restful, not gamified |

Overall session target: **under 60–90 seconds** per game so it reads as a
break, not a new place to lose time — matching the anti-doomscroll guidance
in §3.
